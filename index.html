Wangari Maathai: Nobel Peace Prize Winner

Her mission: Sustainability, Democracy and Peace

Wangari Maathai(1940-2011) had a lot of firsts in her lifetime. She was the first African woman to receive the Nobel Peace Prize. As well as, also being the first female scholar from East Africa to complete a doctorate in Biology and the first female professor EVER in Kenya. Maathai was not only an academic, but also played a very active part in Kenyas' struggle for democracy. She was part fo the opposition to the Moi regime.

In 1977 she started a grass-roots movement aimed at countering the deforestation that was threatening the means of subsistence of the agricultural population. The campaign encouraged women to plant trees in their local environments and to think ecologically. The so-called Green Belt Movement spread to other African countries, and contributed to the planting of over thirty million trees.

Maathai's mobilisation of African women was not limited in its vision to work for sustainable development; she saw tree-planting in a broader perspective which included democracy, women's rights, and international solidarity. In the words of the Nobel Committee: “She thinks globally and acts locally.”

Major Achievements

  • 1971 - Obtains a Doctorate in Biology: Mathaai persued her doctoral studies in Germany and at the University of Nairobi, obtaining a Ph.D. (1971) from the University of Nairobi, where she also taught veterinary anatomy. Later she became chair of the Department of Veterinary Anatomy and an associate professor. In both cases, she was the first Kenyan woman to attain those positions.

  • 1972-1977 Start of Activism : During this time, she also campaigned for equal benefits for the female staff working at the university, going so far as trying to turn the academic staff association of the university into a union, so that they could better negotiate benefits. She was a member of many different charities and volunteeer associations, some of them being; The Nairobi branch of the Kenya Red Cross Society, The Kenya Association of University Women, The Environment Liaison Centre, The National Council of Women of Kenya (NCWK) and The United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP). Her work at these various volunteer associations, helped Maathai come to the conclusion that the root of most of Kenya's problems was environmental degradation.

  • 1977- The Green Belt Movement :Professor Wangari Maathai founded The Green Belt Movement (GBM) under the National Council of Women in Kenya; she launched it to reposnd to the needs of Women in rural Kenya, whose livelihoods were suffering due to environmental degradation. GBM is an environmental organization that empowers communities, particularly women, to conserve the environment and improve livelihoods. These communities reported that their streams were drying up, their food supply was less secure, and they had to walk further and further to get firewood for fuel and fencing. GBM encouraged the women to work together to grow seedlings and plant trees to bind the soil, store rainwater, provide food and firewood, and receive a small monetary token for their work. GBM is still active until this day, where they continue to support communities through tree planting and water harvesting, a wide range of mainstream advocacy related to environmental conservation, fighting gender inequality and campaigning for policies related to climate change. You can learn more about this amazing movement on their website.

  • 2004 Wins Nobel Peace Prize: The Norwegen Nobel Committee awarded Maathai the Nobel Peace Prize for contributing to sustainable development, democracy and peace. Peace on Earth depends on our ability to secure our living environment. They mention her fight to promote ecologically viable social, economic and cultural development in Kenya and Africa. They also her bravery in courageously standing against the Moi oppressive regime in Kenya. Her actions contributed to drawing attention to political oppression – nationally and internationally. She has inspired many in the fight for democratic rights and especially encouraged women to improve their situation. Her part in the Green Belt Movement also contributed to her winning the Nobel peace prize. The committee also mentioned that they believe that Maathai is a strong voice speaking for the best forces in Africa to promote peace and good living conditions on that continent. Maathai was the first African woman honoured with the Nobel Peace Prize. She represented an example and a source of inspiration for everyone in Africa fighting for sustainable development, democracy and peace.
